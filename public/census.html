Mixed heritage people have often gone unrepresented in history.

Though the reality is that most, if not all, people come from a background of mixed heritage, interracial marriages were not legalized across the whole United States until 1967. From the US Census Data it is diffciult to formulate a clear understanding of what social and political changes in regard to race meant for the mixed race and mixed heritage communities - the census did not allow for people to mark themselves with more than one race until the 2000 census and made no allowances for heritage/origins until 2020. Since these changes, the census has grown to accommodate 63 race categories - 57 of those are composed of mixed-race combinations. This shows how difficult it is to categorize humans through race as there are infinite possibilities in the racial composition of individuals.

And, yet, the desire to categorise individuals, with intentional taxonomies, continues. How do systems and taxonomy on the US census reinforce and/or are reinforced by bias/racism? How does the census categories relate to notions of belonging?

Is it possible to bring notions of fluidity and reinvention into a census and embrace an ever-changing state of being in order to subvert institutionalized categorizations?

We invite you to contribute to this dialogue by participating in this Alternative Census.
